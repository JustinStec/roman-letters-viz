Latin Morphological Register & Network Position

Full Corpus: 4,390 Latin letters, 27 collections, 66 BC – 604 AD

1. Morphological Drift Over Time

Passive Voice Ratio — All 4,390 Letters (sampled)
Each dot = one letter, colored by collection. The trend line connects collection means chronologically.
Reading: Passive voice usage rises steadily from ~22% (Cicero, 1st c. BC) to ~38% (Cassiodorus, 6th c.). This reflects the growing dominance of impersonal and bureaucratic registers in late antique correspondence. Notable outlier: Julian the Apostate shows near-zero passive — his texts are almost certainly Greek originals tagged as Latin, and should be excluded from further analysis. Variance matters: The vertical spread within each collection tells us how much an individual author shifts register across letters — wider spread = more code-switching.
Nominal Ratio by Collection
Nouns / (Nouns + Verbs). Higher = more noun-heavy, static prose.
Reading: Cicero's ad Atticum letters are the most verb-heavy (0.35) — fast, action-oriented private correspondence. By the 6th century, Pelagius I reaches 0.61 — nearly two nouns for every verb. The shift tracks the rise of abstract, formulaic chancery Latin.
Case Distribution Over Time
Stacked: how the five main cases redistribute across centuries.
Reading: The accusative share drops from ~40% (Cicero) to ~25% (Gregory). Ablative and genitive shares rise, reflecting the shift from direct syntactic government to prepositional and possessive constructions. This is the morphological fingerprint of the synthetic-to-analytic transition.

2. Network Position & Morphological Conservatism

Network Centrality vs. Morphological Profile
Top 20 authors by betweenness centrality, with their passive ratio and nominal ratio.
Reading: Betweenness centrality measures how often an author lies on the shortest path between other correspondents — high betweenness = a bridge figure connecting otherwise separate social circles. Key finding: Cassiodorus has the highest betweenness (0.41) and is also the most morphologically conformist in his period (cosine distance 0.0006 from baseline). This supports the hypothesis that network bridge figures default to a shared, conservative register. Sidonius, by contrast, is the 2nd-highest bridge but the most distinctive writer — his literary self-consciousness overrides any pressure toward conformity. Bridge score measures the fraction of an author's connections that cross community boundaries.
Conservatism Scores by Period
Cosine distance from period morphological baseline. Lower = more conformist.
Reading: Each bar is one author's distance from the average morphological profile of their period. Augustine is remarkably conformist in both the 4th and 5th centuries. Sidonius is the most distinctive. Cosine distance captures differences in the shape of the morphological distribution, not just magnitude.
Radar: Morphological Fingerprints
Comparing top network bridge figures.
Reading: Overlaid morphological profiles for the three highest-betweenness authors. Cassiodorus (red) has the highest passive ratio and lowest accusative — classic chancery style. Sidonius (purple) shows the most extreme passive usage of all. Pliny (green) is the most balanced and classical.

3. All Collections

All 27 Latin collections ordered chronologically. Passive ratio, nominal ratio, and subjunctive ratio with standard deviations where available.
Reading: Scan left to right to see the temporal progression. The passive and nominal columns generally darken (increase) over time. Exceptions are informative: Venantius Fortunatus (6th c.) has an unusually low passive ratio (0.22) — he's a poet writing in a deliberately classical register. Ruricius of Limoges also bucks the trend (0.26). Genre and literary ambition modulate the chronological signal.
