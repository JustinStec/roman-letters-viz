1. The Landscape: How Latin Changes Over 700 Years

Before asking whether network position shapes morphological register, we need to see the baseline: how does Latin prose change across the seven centuries of this corpus? These charts show every letter in the dataset, plotting four key morphological metrics over time.

Case Distribution Shift
How do grammatical case proportions change over time?
What you're seeing: Each bar shows the mean case distribution for one collection, ordered chronologically. The accusative share drops from ~40% (Cicero) to ~25% (Gregory) while ablative rises. This is the morphological fingerprint of the synthetic-to-analytic transition: bare case constructions give way to prepositional phrases (de + ablative replacing genitive, ad + accusative replacing dative).

2. The Core Question: Does Network Position Predict Morphological Profile?

This is the central hypothesis. Authors who bridge separate social circles (high betweenness centrality) may default to a conservative, shared register — or they may show higher variance. The scatter plot below directly tests this: each dot is one author, with network position on one axis and morphological profile on the other.

Betweenness Centrality vs. Morphological Variance
Do bridge figures write more conservatively — or more variably?
What you're seeing: Each dot is an author. X-axis = betweenness centrality (how much the author bridges separate network communities). Y-axis = within-author standard deviation in passive ratio (how much the author varies across letters). If bridge figures code-switch more, we'd expect a positive correlation (upper-right dots). If they default to a safe register, we'd expect low variance (lower-right). Dot size = letter count.
Author Profiles: Network × Morphology
Who are the bridge figures, and how do they write?
How to read: Sorted by betweenness centrality (network importance). Pass = mean passive ratio. σ = standard deviation (within-author variance). High betweenness + low σ = conformist bridge. High betweenness + high σ = code-switcher. Compare Cassiodorus (bridge, low variance) to Sidonius (bridge, high variance).
Morphological Fingerprints: Bridge vs. Non-Bridge
Do high-betweenness authors have a distinct morphological shape?
What you're seeing: Overlaid morphological profiles for the top 3 bridge figures (Cassiodorus, Sidonius, Pliny) vs. the corpus mean. Where they bulge beyond the mean = their distinctive features. Where they're inside it = features they suppress. Key: Cassiodorus and Sidonius both have elevated passive ratios, but Sidonius pushes further while Cassiodorus stays closer to the mean on other features.

Data: Roman Letters corpus (romanletters.org), CC BY 4.0, DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.19142059. 4,390 Latin letters from v2.1.0 (March 2026), Greek collections excluded.
Tagger: LatinCy 3.9.0 (la_core_web_lg), ~90% morphological accuracy on UD Latin treebanks. Processed on Modal (cloud compute).
Network: 829 nodes, 1,135 directed edges. Betweenness, eigenvector, closeness centrality; Louvain community detection; bridge score = fraction of cross-community edges.
Conservatism: Cosine distance between author mean feature vector and period baseline. Periods: 300–400, 400–500, 500–600 AD (classical periods have too few authors for meaningful baselines).
Caveat: Julian the Apostate shows anomalous values (near-zero passive/subjunctive) — his texts are likely Greek originals, not Latin. Exclude from analysis.
