Case Distribution Shift
How do grammatical case proportions change over time?
What you're seeing: Each bar shows the mean case distribution for one collection, ordered chronologically. The accusative share drops from ~40% (Cicero) to ~25% (Gregory) while ablative rises. This is the morphological fingerprint of the synthetic-to-analytic transition: bare case constructions give way to prepositional phrases (de + ablative replacing genitive, ad + accusative replacing dative).

2. The Core Question: Do Social Connectors Write Different Latin?

Some authors in this corpus wrote to dozens of people across different social circles — bishops, senators, emperors, monks. Others wrote mainly within their own community. The question: do the most connected authors write differently? Specifically, do they default to a "safe," average register — or do they develop their own distinctive style?

Who Are the Bridge Figures?
Authors ranked by how many separate social circles they connect. The bar shows their passive voice ratio; the dashed line marks the period average.
What you're seeing: Authors sorted by betweenness centrality — a network metric that measures how often someone lies on the shortest path between other people. High betweenness = a social connector, someone who bridges otherwise separate groups. The colored bar shows each author's mean passive voice ratio. The dashed vertical line marks the overall corpus average (~0.31). Key pattern: The top bridge figures (Cassiodorus, Sidonius, Pliny) show different strategies. Cassiodorus writes close to the mean — safe, institutional Latin. Sidonius pushes well above it — a deliberately ornate style despite (or because of) his bridging role.
Head-to-Head: Bridge vs. Peripheral Authors
Same period, different network positions. Does the bridge figure write closer to the average?
What you're seeing: Three matched pairs of authors from the same period but different network positions. For each pair, the bars show passive ratio and nominal ratio. The period average is marked with a dashed line. The hypothesis: if network bridging pushes authors toward a shared register, the bridge figure should be closer to the period average than the peripheral author. What we find: Cassiodorus (biggest bridge) writes higher passive than the period mean — he doesn't default to average, he writes in a distinctive chancery style. But his nominal ratio is lower than the period mean, closer to the overall average. The picture is mixed: bridge figures aren't simply "more average," but they may converge on specific features.

Data: Roman Letters corpus (romanletters.org), CC BY 4.0, DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.19142059. 4,390 Latin letters from v2.1.0 (March 2026), Greek collections excluded.
Tagger: LatinCy 3.9.0 (la_core_web_lg), ~90% morphological accuracy on UD Latin treebanks. Processed on Modal (cloud compute).
Network: 829 nodes, 1,135 directed edges. Betweenness, eigenvector, closeness centrality; Louvain community detection; bridge score = fraction of cross-community edges.
Conservatism: Cosine distance between author mean feature vector and period baseline. Periods: 300–400, 400–500, 500–600 AD (classical periods have too few authors for meaningful baselines).
Caveat: Julian the Apostate shows anomalous values (near-zero passive/subjunctive) — his texts are likely Greek originals, not Latin. Exclude from analysis.
